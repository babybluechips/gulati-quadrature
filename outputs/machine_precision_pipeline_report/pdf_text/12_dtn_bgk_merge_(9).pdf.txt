1.4. Positioning within the literature. The two halves of the statement are individually classical and individually recent. On the probabilistic side, the chain β ↔ ζ( 12 ) ↔ Gaussian-walk
maximum is the content of [5, 10, 4, 7, 8]; what those works do not do is identify ζ( 12 ) with a
boundary spectral invariant of a concrete operator. On the operator side, the identification (2) of
Qn with the DtN operator, the exact spectrum (3), and the bridge decomposition of the trapezoidal error into bulk, local, and remainder channels are proved self-contained in Section 2; what
that analysis leaves open is the regularized constant that the local channel carries to its on-curve
limit. The present paper supplies exactly the missing link: the regularized constant is ζ( 12 ), and
it is the BGK correction. The mechanism that makes the identification rigorous — rather than a
numerical coincidence between two appearances of the same decimal — is Spitzer’s combinatorial
identity [11, 12], which converts the discrete Gaussian-walk maximum into a half-integer power
sum and exhibits β literally as that sum’s endpoint defect. The novelty is the unification: a
single operator Qn whose discrete-to-continuum adjustment, its near-singular quadrature error,
and the financial continuity correction are three readings of one Euler–Maclaurin boundary term.
A second, orthogonal novelty (Section 3) is that this operator is not given by fiat but generated
locally: an all-orders jet identity reconstructs its entries, and the whole point configuration, from
the Taylor jet of the defining polynomial at each node, so the global spectral object of Theorem 1
is the long-range shadow of strictly local data. The Basel limit h2 (Qn )ii → 2ζ(2) closes the loop,
placing the inverse-square diagonal on the even-integer rung of the same ζ-ladder.
