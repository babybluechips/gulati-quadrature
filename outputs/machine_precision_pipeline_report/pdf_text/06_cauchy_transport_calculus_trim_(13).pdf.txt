An Inverse-Square Boundary Laplacian for Near-Singular Quadrature
Rick Gulati
University of Pennsylvania
rkgulati@upenn.edu

Abstract
Boundary-integral methods represent the solution of an elliptic problem as a layer potential — an integral over the
boundary against an unknown density. Evaluating it at points near the boundary, the close-evaluation problem, has been
the standing bottleneck of the field for four decades: a fixed high-order quadrature rule is accurate far away but loses all
of its digits once the target lies within one node spacing of the boundary, where the kernel becomes sharply peaked.
We show that this difficulty is governed by a single intrinsic operator on the curve — the inverse-square boundary
Laplacian Q, the graph Laplacian whose weight between two boundary points is the inverse square of the distance
between them — and that Q is the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator up to a bounded term. On the circle Q is
circulant with the integer spectrum λm = m(n − m)/2, applied by the FFT in O(n log n) time and conditioned as O(n),
against O(n3 ) for regularized hypersingular assembly. Diagonalizing Q evaluates the layer potential as a modal sum in
which the target enters only through one scalar, so on analytic data the rule reaches machine precision uniformly in
the distance to the boundary — verified to 50 digits and carried to non-circular curves by a closed-form conformal
pullback. Because the spectrum is an exact quadratic, the same closed form inverts the discrete-to-continuum defect
exactly, curing near-resonance Helmholtz and Steklov evaluation, and the construction extends through a corner by a
Hurwitz-zeta correction. In a direct comparison with quadrature by expansion the fixed rule is distance-uniform where
QBX degrades as the target approaches, is competitive at the closest distances at a fraction of the nodes, and stays
defined at cusps where the QBX expansion centre does not exist. The distance-uniformity is a property of the analytic
class, and we fence it as such.

1.1. A taxonomy of boundary-quadrature error
“Quadrature error” on a boundary is not one phenomenon
but several, with distinct sources and scalings, and the
operator organizes them: each is a feature of Q or its
discretization. Table 1 is the map; reading down it is
reading the paper. The near-singular error is the central
case because it is the one the classical theory cannot
name; the discretization and weak-singular errors are
the two channels into which it splits at the mesh scale;
the hypersingular entry is the same operator read as a
boundary-element matrix; and the corner entry is the
algebraic degradation the same residue calculus controls.

Expansion- and source-based methods. Quadrature by

Each discretizes the integral and, in resolving one

3. The operator behind the breakdown

The benchmark for optimality is supplied by approximation theory, not by comparison with the previous rule.
For a fixed smoothness class F the worst-case error of
the best n-point rule is, up to the choice of admissible
information, a Gel’fand width of the layer-potential functional restricted to F [24, 21]; the theory of n-widths
identifies the worst-case error of the best n-point rule
with a width of the unit ball of F under the functional
and characterizes the rules that attain it. For analytic
classes the relevant geometry is potential-theoretic: the
optimal nodes are the Fekete points of Γ for the exterior
equilibrium measure, and the attainable rate is governed
by the conformal modulus [28]. The framework of optimal recovery and information-based complexity [23]
fixes the sense in which a rule is best: it minimizes the
radius of information over the class.
Against this benchmark, the periodic trapezoidal rule
is the classical near-miss. On analytic densities, away
from the boundary, it attains the optimal exponential rate
up to a universal constant [33, 4]. But the optimal rate off
the curve is governed by the conformal distance from target to boundary, while the trapezoidal exponent 2πnδ /L
sees only the Euclidean distance, and the two diverge as
δ → 0. That gap is made precise in §7.

4. Dirichlet-to-Neumann identification
On a general curve Q loses its circulant form but keeps
its symbolic identity. We establish the identification in
full, and are deliberately economical about the machinery:
the only property of the remainder that any later inference
uses is that it is a bounded self-adjoint operator on L2 ,
and that is exactly what we prove — at C2 regularity, by
an elementary bounded-kernel estimate, with no appeal
to a classical (polyhomogeneous) symbol expansion. The
stronger statement that the remainder is a classical orderzero pseudodifferential operator holds when Γ ∈ C∞ , but
it is not needed and we do not assume it (Remark 4.8).

9.2. Head-to-head against QBX
Quadrature by expansion (QBX) is the standard closeevaluation method: it forms a local expansion of the
layer potential about a centre placed off the boundary and
evaluates the expansion at the near target. We compare it
with the fixed Q-rule on the single-layer potential, against
a 65,536-node reference, under two protocols: at matched
resolution (both at n = 512), and with QBX refined by its
usual upsampling to n = 4096 at order 50. Table 3 shows

9.5. Off-circle validation and the analytic-class fence
Table 6 carries the comparison off the circle, n = 512
source nodes against an n = 65536 reference. On analytic
curves reached by the conformal pullback (§5.3) — an
ellipse and a rounded square — the spectral Q error is
flat in the standoff δ (a few ×10−5 across two decades)
while the trapezoidal rule degrades by one to two orders.
The star polygon is the boundary of the class: it is only
Lipschitz, the pullback is not analytic at its vertices, and
the spectral error plateaus at ∼ 4.5 × 10−3 — still flat
in δ , but at a corner-limited floor rather than machine
precision. Uniformity in δ is a property of the analytic
class, and the table shows both sides of that line.

10. Conclusion
One operator runs through the paper: the inverse-square
chord Laplacian Q, the Hessian of the log-discriminant
along the boundary, with its kernel forced rather than
chosen (Proposition 3.1, Lemma 6.1). Three identifications make it useful for near-singular quadrature. It
is a graph Laplacian with closed-form integer spectrum
λm = m(n − m)/2 on the circle, giving an O(n log n) FFT
functional calculus and O(n) conditioning where regularized hypersingular assembly costs O(n3 ) (§3, §7). It is, on
any C1,1 curve, the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator up to a bounded self-adjoint term, Q = πΛΓ + K0 ,
established by an elementary estimate together with the
resulting Weyl law (§4). And because its spectrum is an
exact quadratic in the mode, the continuum
