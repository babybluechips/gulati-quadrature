Abstract
Evaluating a logarithmic layer potential at a target near the curve that carries it is a central, decades-old bottleneck of
boundary-integral computation, setting the achievable accuracy of boundary-element and Nystr√∂m solvers throughout
acoustics, electromagnetics, viscous flow, and potential theory. Forty years of progress have taken the form of ever-better
quadrature rules (corrected weights, expansion centers, swapped singularities, interpolated densities), each resolving
one regime and exposing the next. We instead ask what the optimal rule is, and exhibit the operator that attains it.
We change the object of study from the rule to a complex boundary Laplacian behind it. Its kernel is not chosen but
forced, arising as the Hessian of the log-discriminant of the node configuration. On equispaced nodes of the circle it is
circulant with an exact integer spectrum őĽm = m(n ‚ąí m)/2, so the fast Fourier transform evaluates the potential and
its inverse in O(n log n) time, and as a hypersingular discretization it is O(n)-conditioned against O(n3 ) for standard
assembly, with no regularization. To a bounded zeroth-order term the operator is the Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator of
the enclosed domain, hence intrinsic to the geometry rather than to any rule; and because its singular part is a function
of one fixed transform, every analytic Jordan curve reduces to that transform plus bounded corrections.
The main results are two optimality statements. First, among admissible Euclidean-covariant chord-graph discretizations of the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator, the complex Laplacian is the unique one with vanishing principalsymbol defect: it is forced, not convenient. Second, the spectral rule it generates attains the information-theoretic
minimax error for n-sample layer-potential quadrature on analytic Jordan curves, őļn ‚Čć C(őď) n‚ąí1 e‚ąí(n/2)(log ŌĀ+őīő¶ (x)) , with
the rate set by the conformal collar width log ŌĀ and the conformal target depth őīő¶ (x), and attained on the conformal
image of equispaced nodes, the asymptotic Fekete configuration of the curve. The periodic trapezoidal rule, by contrast,
attains this rate only where Euclidean and conformal distance agree and is minimax-suboptimal off the curve; a single
remark records the gap.
The same residue calculus carries through a corner, where a Mellin/Kondrat‚Äôev exponent and a Hurwitz-zeta weight
take the place of the smooth profile and the half-order endpoint constant of barrier-option theory reappears as the crack
case. At four thousand sample points and a target one part in a million from the curve, the classical rule holds four
digits; the optimal rule here holds sixteen, uniformly in the distance to the boundary.

2.1. Near-singular quadrature: four families of rules
Near-singular evaluation has driven four decades of
method development, and the present work stands in the
same relation to all four families.
On-curve specialized quadratures. Kress logarithmic
splitting and the Kapur‚ÄďRokhlin and Alpert corrected
rules [11] attain high order on őď but degrade off it, because the analytic splitting of the singularity does not
extend to a nearby exterior target.
Local corrections and coordinate maps. Beale‚ÄďLai [5]
and the Telles and Hayami transformations regularize
the peaked integrand at fixed algebraic order; they are
adequate for mild nearness but not for the őī /h ‚Üí 0 limit,
where the algebraic order caps the accuracy.
Complex-analytic close evaluation. Helsing‚ÄďOjala [9],
Barnett‚Äôs compensated rule [3], and singularity swapping [2] reach near-machine precision at any distance
in two dimensions by exploiting the Cauchy/Schwarz
structure of the planar kernel, at the price of machinery
specific to that setting and, for tensor kernels, delicate
cancellation.
Expansion- and source-based methods. Quadrature by
expansion [10, 1], density interpolation [18], and quadrature by fundamental solutions [4] separate the singularity
through an auxiliary representation, with sharp a posteriori control |EQBX (x)| ‚Č§ Cőď ŌĄ(c, x) p+1 [1]; the expansion
order or source placement must be tuned per target, and
cost grows as targets approach.
Each of these discretizes the integral and, in resolving
one regime, exposes the next. None isolates an intrinsic
object on the curve whose spectrum controls the error,
and none is accompanied by a proof that it attains the best
possible rate.
There is a further structural difference, and on modern
hardware it is not a minor one. Each of these families
resolves the near-singular regime through local, datadependent control flow: an expansion order and center
chosen per target, a singularity swap selected by configuration, a panel split or coordinate map switched on by a
proximity test. That branching is what makes the methods accurate, but it carries two costs. The rule becomes a

2.2. Optimal recovery: what ‚Äúoptimal‚ÄĚ means here
The benchmark for optimality is supplied by approximation theory, not by comparison with the previous rule. For
a fixed smoothness class F the minimax error (4) is, up
to the choice of admissible information, a Gel‚Äôfand width
of the layer-potential functional restricted to F [20]; the
theory of n-widths identifies the worst-case error of the
best n-point rule with a width of the unit ball of F under
the functional, and characterizes the rules that attain it.
For analytic classes the relevant geometry is potentialtheoretic: the optimal nodes are the Fekete points of őď for
the exterior equilibrium measure, and the attainable rate
is governed by the conformal modulus [22]. The general
framework of optimal recovery and information-based
complexity [17] fixes the precise sense in which a rule
can be called best: it minimizes the radius of information
over the class.
This minimax criterion has a concrete few-point ancestor. Birkhoff and Gulati [19] fixed the optimality of a
compact discretization of the Laplace source problem not
by comparison with existing stencils but by the lowestdegree harmonic polynomial no stencil of a given node
budget can reproduce: the extremal Re z4 that every fivepoint stencil annihilates pins O(h2 ) as the best attainable
order, and Re z8 does the same for nine points at O(h4 ).

fooling function in the orthogonal complement of the sampled data, certifying that no admissible rule of the given
budget can do better, transposed from finite-difference
stencils to near-singular boundary quadrature. We take it

Generality. The construction is not special to the circle.

on non-equispaced nodes through fast-multipole or H matrix application of Q, at the usual O(n log n) cost.
