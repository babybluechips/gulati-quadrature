A Complex Laplacian for Optimal Quadrature
Rick Gulati
University of Pennsylvania
rkgulati@upenn.edu

Abstract
Evaluating a logarithmic layer potential at a target near the curve that carries it is a central, decades-old bottleneck of
boundary-integral computation, setting the achievable accuracy of boundary-element and Nyström solvers throughout
acoustics, electromagnetics, viscous flow, and potential theory. Forty years of progress have taken the form of ever-better
quadrature rules (corrected weights, expansion centers, swapped singularities, interpolated densities), each resolving
one regime and exposing the next. We instead ask what the optimal rule is, and exhibit the operator that attains it.
We change the object of study from the rule to a complex boundary Laplacian behind it. Its kernel is not chosen but
forced, arising as the Hessian of the log-discriminant of the node configuration. On equispaced nodes of the circle it is
circulant with an exact integer spectrum λm = m(n − m)/2, so the fast Fourier transform evaluates the potential and
its inverse in O(n log n) time, and as a hypersingular discretization it is O(n)-conditioned against O(n3 ) for standard
assembly, with no regularization. To a bounded zeroth-order term the operator is the Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator of
the enclosed domain, hence intrinsic to the geometry rather than to any rule; and because its singular part is a function
of one fixed transform, every analytic Jordan curve reduces to that transform plus bounded corrections.
The main results are two optimality statements. First, among admissible Euclidean-covariant chord-graph discretizations of the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator, the complex Laplacian is the unique one with vanishing principalsymbol defect: it is forced, not convenient. Second, the spectral rule it generates attains the information-theoretic
minimax error for n-sample layer-potential quadrature on analytic Jordan curves, κn ≍ C(Γ) n−1 e−(n/2)(log ρ+δΦ (x)) , with
the rate set by the conformal collar width log ρ and the conformal target depth δΦ (x), and attained on the conformal
image of equispaced nodes, the asymptotic Fekete configuration of the curve. The periodic trapezoidal rule, by contrast,
attains this rate only where Euclidean and conformal distance agree and is minimax-suboptimal off the curve; a single
remark records the gap.
The same residue calculus carries through a corner, where a Mellin/Kondrat’ev exponent and a Hurwitz-zeta weight
take the place of the smooth profile and the half-order endpoint constant of barrier-option theory reappears as the crack
case. At four thousand sample points and a target one part in a million from the curve, the classical rule holds four
digits; the optimal rule here holds sixteen, uniformly in the distance to the boundary.

2.1. Near-singular quadrature: four families of rules
Near-singular evaluation has driven four decades of
method development, and the present work stands in the
same relation to all four families.
On-curve specialized quadratures. Kress logarithmic
splitting and the Kapur–Rokhlin and Alpert corrected
rules [11] attain high order on Γ but degrade off it, because the analytic splitting of the singularity does not
extend to a nearby exterior target.
Local corrections and coordinate maps. Beale–Lai [5]
and the Telles and Hayami transformations regularize
the peaked integrand at fixed algebraic order; they are
adequate for mild nearness but not for the δ /h → 0 limit,
where the algebraic order caps the accuracy.
Complex-analytic close evaluation. Helsing–Ojala [9],
Barnett’s compensated rule [3], and singularity swapping [2] reach near-machine precision at any distance
in two dimensions by exploiting the Cauchy/Schwarz
structure of the planar kernel, at the price of machinery
specific to that setting and, for tensor kernels, delicate
cancellation.
Expansion- and source-based methods. Quadrature by
expansion [10, 1], density interpolation [18], and quadrature by fundamental solutions [4] separate the singularity
through an auxiliary representation, with sharp a posteriori control |EQBX (x)| ≤ CΓ τ(c, x) p+1 [1]; the expansion
order or source placement must be tuned per target, and
cost grows as targets approach.
Each of these discretizes the integral and, in resolving
one regime, exposes the next. None isolates an intrinsic
object on the curve whose spectrum controls the error,
and none is accompanied by a proof that it attains the best
possible rate.
There is a further structural difference, and on modern
hardware it is not a minor one. Each of these families
resolves the near-singular regime through local, datadependent control flow: an expansion order and center
chosen per target, a singularity swap selected by configuration, a panel split or coordinate map switched on by a
proximity test. That branching is what makes the methods accurate, but it carries two costs. The rule becomes a
piecewise object, non-differentiable in the target and the

2.3. Why not Gauss–Legendre?
Gauss–Legendre is the natural challenger: it is the classically optimal rule for the interval problem, and panelbased Gauss–Legendre is the workhorse of boundaryelement practice. It is worth saying precisely why it is
the wrong optimum here, because the comparison is what
makes the present rule’s optimality meaningful.
Gauss–Legendre with n nodes is exact on polynomials
of degree ≤ 2n − 1, and on an integrand analytic inside a
Bernstein ellipse of parameter R > 1 it converges at rate
O(R−2n ) [7]. This is optimal when the integrand is fixed
and its analyticity ellipse is fixed. The near-singular layer
potential is neither. Three structural facts separate the
two, and the benchmark of §9 (Table 6, Gauss column)
confirms each:
(1) The Bernstein ellipse pinches at the target. For a
target at Euclidean distance δ , the parametrized integrand
t 7→ log |x − γ(t)| has its nearest complex singularity at
distance ≍ δ off the real axis, so its Bernstein parameter
is R = 1 + O(δ ) and the Gauss rate R−2n → 1 collapses as
δ → 0. The Q-rule never parametrizes the singular kernel:
it diagonalizes the operator and evaluates the closed-form
modal sum (49), so no ellipse pinch occurs and the rate is

2.2. Optimal recovery: what “optimal” means here
The benchmark for optimality is supplied by approximation theory, not by comparison with the previous rule. For
a fixed smoothness class F the minimax error (4) is, up
to the choice of admissible information, a Gel’fand width
of the layer-potential functional restricted to F [20]; the
theory of n-widths identifies the worst-case error of the
best n-point rule with a width of the unit ball of F under
the functional, and characterizes the rules that attain it.
For analytic classes the relevant geometry is potentialtheoretic: the optimal nodes are the Fekete points of Γ for
the exterior equilibrium measure, and the attainable rate
is governed by the conformal modulus [22]. The general
framework of optimal recovery and information-based
complexity [17] fixes the precise sense in which a rule
can be called best: it minimizes the radius of information
over the class.
This minimax criterion has a concrete few-point ancestor. Birkhoff and Gulati [19] fixed the optimality of a
compact discretization of the Laplace source problem not
by comparison with existing stencils but by the lowestdegree harmonic polynomial no stencil of a given node
budget can reproduce: the extremal Re z4 that every fivepoint stencil annihilates pins O(h2 ) as the best attainable
order, and Re z8 does the same for nine points at O(h4 ).
That is the logic the lower bound of §6.2 runs: an explicit
fooling function in the orthogonal complement of the sampled data, certifying that no admissible rule of the given
budget can do better, transposed from finite-difference
stencils to near-singular boundary quadrature. We take it
as the methodological precedent for the optimality criterion adopted here, the extremal-function counterpart of

6. Optimal quadrature
This is the flagship of the paper. We prove two optimality
statements. The first is at the level of the operator: among
all admissible discretizations of the boundary Dirichletto-Neumann operator, the complex Laplacian Q is the
unique one with no leading defect, so its kernel is forced
by optimality and not merely convenient. The second is
at the level of the rule: the spectral Q-rule attains the
minimax error (4) on analytic Jordan curves, i.e. no nsample linear rule can do asymptotically better. Together
they justify the title: the complex Laplacian is the operator
behind optimal quadrature.

8.3. Restored-order convergence
The barrier of Remark 8.13 is exactly what the vertex
correction removes. Subtracting the rungs below a chosen
order lifts the convergence rate from the corner-limited
λmin to the regularity of what remains.

Remark 8.11 (What the sampling defect can and cannot
supply). One may ask whether the BGK ladder alone,
applied to Qn , already delivers the corner Dirichlet-toNeumann operator. It does not, for a structural reason.
The BGK weights are a sampling defect: they refine
Qn toward its own continuum limit π|∂ | as h → 0. A
corner does not perturb that limit by a sampling artifact,
it changes the continuum operator itself, the symbol of
ΛΓ ceasing to be |ξ | and the Steklov spectrum clustering
near π/2α, a geometry effect (local chord and curvature
data) generated by a different mechanism and sharing
no rung with the half-integer sampling family. What
the sampling sector does supply is exactly what nearsingular evaluation needs: the trapezoidal defect of the
singular density (Theorem 8.7), in which geometry fixes
the exponent λqℓ and the weight ζ (1 − λqℓ , β ) converts
it to a correction. The corner DtN operator is a separate
object, now explicit as the wedge matrix (51), and what
a sampling correction cannot supply is its off-diagonal
face coupling, the diagonal channels µ± being the wedge
analogue of |ξ |; that coupling is not required for the
quadrature problem here.

14. Conclusion
The near-singular barrier in layer-potential quadrature is
removed by a single operator that is, moreover, provably
optimal. The inverse-square Q has a kernel forced by the
log-discriminant Hessian (7), an exact integer spectrum
λm = m(n−m)/2 on S1 with O(n log n) calculus (11), and
the Dirichlet-to-Neumann identification Q = πΛΓ + K0
on any C3 curve (14); the universal pullback carries the
circulant calculus to any analytic Jordan curve through
bounded corrections. Optimality then holds at both levels: among admissible Euclidean-covariant chord-graph
discretizations of the boundary DtN operator, Q is the
unique one with zero principal-symbol defect (Theorem 6.2), and the spectral Q-rule attains the informationtheoretic minimax error κn ≍ C(Γ)n−1 e−(n/2)(log ρ+δΦ (x))
on analytic Jordan curves (Theorem 6.4), on the conformal image of equispaced nodes; the trapezoidal rule
attains this only where Euclidean and conformal distance coincide. Underneath the rate, the near-singular
error has a clean three-channel anatomy (§7) that extends to corners (§8) through a vertex channel in which a
Mellin/Kondrat’ev exponent sets the order and a Hurwitzzeta weight the coefficient, with BGK as the crack case.
The benchmarks confirm the analysis end to end: δ uniform machine precision where the classical rule holds
four digits (Table 6), O(n) versus O(n3 ) conditioning
(Table 7), exactness of the local profile, and the same
crossover on non-circular geometries (Table 9).
