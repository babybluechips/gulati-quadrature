Abstract
We introduce a meshless framework for optimizing the shapes of two-dimensional closed
boundaries to extremize kernel-based coupling functionals subject to geometric constraints. The
boundaries are represented as truncated Fourier series in the parameter domain, yielding a
finite-dimensional optimization problem whose design variables are spectral coefficients. We
derive closed-form expressions for the area functional and analytic shape gradients of the coupling
objective through the Fourier basis, avoiding both mesh generation and finite-difference gradient
approximations. Constraints on enclosed area, inter-component separation, curvature, and
spectral roughness are enforced through an augmented Lagrangian method with L-BFGS inner
solves. We prove that the area of a Fourier-parameterized boundary admits an exact quadratic
form in the coefficients, establish well-posedness of the regularized coupling functional, and provide
convergence estimates for the trapezoidal quadrature on smooth periodic integrands. Numerical
experiments on two-conductor coupling maximization demonstrate that the framework recovers
the expected parallel-plate deformation, satisfies area constraints to machine precision, and
passes component-wise gradient verification at O(10−9 ) relative error. The entire optimization—
from boundary representation through converged shape—operates without any volumetric
discretization.

Introduction

Shape optimization problems arise in electrostatics, fluid mechanics, acoustics, and structural design
wherever the geometry of a domain boundary is a design variable [16, 1, 14]. Classical approaches
discretize the domain with a finite-element or boundary-element mesh, solve the governing PDE,
compute shape sensitivities via an adjoint method, and update the mesh [9]. This pipeline introduces
several well-known difficulties: mesh generation is expensive, mesh quality degrades under large
deformations, remeshing introduces interpolation error, and the entire framework couples the physics
solver tightly to the geometry representation.
In this paper we propose an alternative: a boundary-only, meshless framework in which the
geometry is parameterized directly by Fourier coefficients, the physics objective is a boundary

The key observation is that for a broad class of kernel-based coupling functionals—including the
logarithmic kernel that generates the two-dimensional Laplace Green’s function—the objective, its
gradient, and the relevant geometric constraints (area, separation, curvature) can all be expressed as
integrals over the boundary alone. When the boundary is represented as a truncated Fourier series,
these integrals become functions of the spectral coefficients that can be differentiated in closed form.

Contributions

Our main contributions are:
1. A complete meshless shape optimization framework in which every quantity—objective, gradient, constraints, and regularizers—is computed from boundary data alone (sections 2 and 3).
2. An exact, closed-form expression for the area of a Fourier-parameterized boundary as a
quadratic form in the spectral coefficients, together with its analytic Jacobian (theorem 2.2).
3. Analytic shape gradients of the kernel coupling functional with respect to Fourier coefficients,
derived via the chain rule through the position and speed Jacobians of the parameterization
(section 3).

1.2

Related work

Shape optimization has a long history; see [16, 5] for comprehensive treatments. Boundary-integral
approaches to shape sensitivity analysis were developed in [3, 6], and Fourier-based boundary
parameterizations have been used for inverse problems in [12, 13] and shape optimization in [8, 6].
Our work differs in providing a fully self-contained optimization stack—from parameterization
through constrained optimizer—with analytic gradients, rather than relying on external PDE solvers.
The kernel coupling functional we consider is closely related to the mutual capacitance and
Neumann–Poincaré operator studied in [11, 2]. The connection between spectral representations
and capacity has been explored in [7, 10].

L-BFGS inner solver

The inner minimization on line 4 of section 5.1 is performed by L-BFGS with memory M = 10 and
backtracking line search. The L-BFGS direction is computed via the standard two-loop recursion
[15]: given correction pairs {(si , yi )}m
i=1 where si = pi+1 − pi and yi = ∇Li+1 − ∇Li , the search
direction d = −Hm ∇L is computed without forming Hm explicitly.
The initial Hessian approximation is H0 = γI with γ = sTm−1 ym−1 / ∥ym−1 ∥2 . To ensure stability,
we use an adaptive initial step size αinit = min(α0 , 1/ ∥d∥) and clip gradients with ∥∇L∥ > 106 .

5.3

Convergence safeguards

Our implementation includes several safeguards motivated by the geometry of the problem:
1. Geometry validity check. After each inner solve, we verify that 10−6 < |A(pℓ )| < 104 for
all components. If violated, we reject the step and increase ρ.
2. Best-solution tracking. We maintain the best coefficient vector p∗ seen so far, measured
by a combination of constraint satisfaction and objective value. The final output is p∗ rather
than the last iterate.
3. Gradient clipping. Within the augmented Lagrangian subproblem, gradients are clipped to
norm ≤ 106 to prevent step explosion when the penalty parameter is large.
4. Objective normalization. The objective is divided by J0 to balance its scale against the
constraint penalties.

Computational complexity

The dominant cost per optimization iteration is the kernel coupling evaluation and gradient
computation. For Nb = 2 boundaries with nq quadrature points each:
1. Kernel matrix assembly: O(n2q ).
2. Gradient (position + speed terms): O(n2q · nc ) via the Jacobian contractions in (17).
3. Area constraint: O(m) (closed form).
4. Gap constraint: O(n2s ) (pairwise distances).
The total per-iteration cost is O(n2q · nc ). Since nc = O(m) and we typically have nq ≫ m, the
kernel evaluation dominates.
For the experiments in this paper (nq = 96, nc = 52), the wall time is under two minutes for

Discussion and future work

We have presented a complete, self-contained framework for meshless boundary shape optimization.
The key design decisions that enable this approach are: (i) Fourier parameterization, which makes
the boundary linear in its design variables; (ii) boundary-only objectives, which eliminate volumetric
discretization; and (iii) analytic gradients through the basis function Jacobians, which avoid finitedifference approximations.
The framework is extensible along several axes. The kernel function K is a plug-in; any kernel
with known gradients can be used. The Fourier parameterization can be replaced by B-splines or
NURBS while retaining the same gradient structure. The augmented Lagrangian can be replaced
by SQP or interior-point methods.
Limitations. The current framework is limited to two-dimensional, fixed-topology problems.
Extension to three dimensions would require replacing the Fourier series with spherical harmonics
or subdivision surfaces. The kernel coupling objective is a surrogate for the true capacitance; for
applications requiring precise capacitance values, the full BIE solve is necessary. Finally, the O(n2q )
kernel evaluation limits scalability to problems with thousands of boundary degrees of freedom
without fast methods.

11

Conclusions

We introduced a meshless framework for two-dimensional boundary shape optimization that operates
entirely through spectral coefficients and boundary integrals. The Fourier area formula (6) is
exact, the kernel coupling gradient (17) is analytic, and the entire optimization pipeline—from
parameterization through augmented Lagrangian convergence—requires no volumetric mesh, no
PDE assembly, and no finite-difference gradients. Numerical experiments confirm gradient accuracy
at 10−9 , exact area preservation, and physically correct parallel-plate deformation of coupled
conductors. The framework provides a foundation for boundary-centric optimization of problems
governed by kernel-based interactions.
