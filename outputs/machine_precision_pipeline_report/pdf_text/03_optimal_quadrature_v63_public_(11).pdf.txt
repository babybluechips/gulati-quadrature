THE LOG-DISCRIMINANT HESSIAN:
SPECTRUM, CONIC COORDINATES, AND BOUNDARY QUADRATURE
RICK GULATI
Abstract. For a configuration V = (v1 , . . . , vn ) of distinct complex points, the log-discriminant
Hessian Q(V ) := − 12 ∇2V log |∆(V )| is a polynomial-algebraic operator with four foundational
identifications: closed-form spectrum λm (Qn ) = m(n − m)/2 on equispaced nodes (Theorem 2.2), principal-symbol equality QΓ = πΛΓ + K0 with K0 ∈ Ψ0 (Γ) on C 3 closed planar
curves (Theorem 3.1), the identification Q = π(−∆S 1 )1/2 as the fractional Laplacian on the
circle (Corollary 3.2), and the spectral zeta identity Tn (s) → 2ζ(s) on C \ Z≥1 at rate O(1/n)
(Theorem 4.1).
These foundations support an algebraic encoding of shape and scale through conic coordinates on the Dirichlet spectrum: the spectral map Σ : Ω 7→ (λ1 , λ2 , . . .) factors through
the moduli quotient D/ ∼ times R+ , with eigenvalue ratios pinning shape and one absolute eigenvalue pinning scale (Theorem 5.1); given (λ1 , D2 λ1 ) on piecewise C 2,α simply connected planar domains, Ω is determined up to Euclidean motion via the dressed factorization
Hres = −2M∂ν u1 QΓ M∂ν u1 +E and piecewise Frenet reconstruction (Theorem 6.4); once shape
is identified, area drops out of the radial coordinate as Area(Ω) = CQ ([Ω])/λ1 (Ω)2 (Corollary 5.2).
The boundary quadrature corollary is information-theoretic
optimality. For n-sample linR
ear rules approximating layer potentials I(x) = Γ σ(y) log |x − y| ds(y) on analytic Jordan
curves Γ with σ in the Hardy class Fρ , the minimax rate is κn (Γ, x; Fρ ) ≍ C(Γ) n−1 e−(n/2)(log ρ+δΦ (x))
where δΦ (x) = log |Φ(x)| is the exterior conformal distance (Theorem 7.2). The spectral
Q-rule on the conformal pullback of equispaced nodes attains the rate up to a universal
constant; near-singular evaluation (nδ ≲ 1) is governed by the Quadrature-Curvature Law
(Theorem 7.1). The framework is linearizable: every analytic Jordan curve pulls back to S 1
under the exterior Riemann map, and Q is the unique scale-phase intertwiner of complex
exponentiation (Theorem 8.1).
A reference implementation in the companion package GulatiLaplacian [22] realizes the
framework around the single unifying mechanism: every analytic Jordan curve quadrature
problem reduces, via the exterior Riemann map Φ and Theorem 7.3, to the same circle problem on S 1 controlled by QS 1 = π(−∆S 1 )1/2 . Across four representative shape classes — S 1 ,
star-shaped smooth (3-petal flower, perturbed ellipse), non-star-shaped smooth (teardrop),
and true-corner (unit square) — the same algorithm is applied against four different precomputed Φs. In fp64: machine precision uniformly in δ on S 1 ; ∼ 4 × 10−14 uniformly in
δ ∈ [10−6 , 10−1 ] on the flower and ellipse; ∼ 6 × 10−7 on the teardrop; graceful algebraic
degradation on the square with uniform-in-δ behavior preserved (4–5× better than classical
periodic trapezoidal at δ = 10−6 , two orders of magnitude better near a corner). The algorithm is arithmetic-agnostic: an adaptive gate escalates to mpmath multiprecision when the
fp64 two-grid estimator hits floor, certifying 93–95 digits at ρ − 1 = 10−40 on the exteriorconformal benchmark in 63–78 ms per target and ∼ 10−149 at ϵ = 10−80 on the adaptive path.
The arithmetic tier governs the floor; the algorithm governs the rate.
Method. Think geometrically, compute numerically, prove analytically, certify algebraically.
Pictures (the discriminant cone, the log-polar cylinder, conic pencils, Cassini ovals) carry
intuition; numerics across fp64 and multiprecision tiers confirm the predicted spectrum, the
O(n log n) functional calculus, and the uniform-in-δ near-singular regime on shapes from
S 1 through true corners; pseudodifferential calculus, Mellin contour integration, and SeeleyHörmander zeta theory provide the analytic proofs; algebraic identities like λm = m(n−m)/2,
Qn 1 = 0, and detζ QS 1 = 2 hold without floating-point slack.

7.4. Comparison with Gauss. Gauss-Legendre quadrature with n nodes integrates polynomials of degree ≤ 2n − 1 exactly, and on analytic integrands inside a Bernstein ellipse of
parameter ρ > 1 achieves rate O(ρ−2n ) [17]. For matched analyticity radii δΦ ≍ 2 log ρ, the
engine’s rate matches Gauss to leading order. The engine improves on Gauss structurally in
three ways: (i) the exponent in (27) is target-dependent, δΦ (x) rather than global log ρ; (ii) the
engine is intrinsic to closed curves and non-tensor-product geometry, where Gauss requires extrinsic parametrization; (iii) the engine diagonalizes the boundary integral operator QΓ rather
than polynomial integration, delivering layer potentials via the O(n log n) functional calculus.
8. Linearization via complex exponentiation
The pullback to S 1 via the exterior Riemann map linearizes the framework. The log-polar
cylinder C = R × T is the universal cover of C× via exp : (ξ, η) 7→ eξ+iη ; the scale axis ξ and
phase axis η carry commuting flows that exp-correspond to dilation and rotation on C× .
Theorem 8.1 (Scale-phase intertwiner). Let A be a self-adjoint operator on L2 (T). Suppose:
(i) A is translation-invariant (commutes with rotations of T);
(ii) the heat semigroup e−tA admits a scale-phase factorization on C that lifts via exp to the
Poisson semigroup on D;
(iii) A has principal symbol π|ξ|;
(iv) the fundamental Fourier mode is normalized as a π-eigenvector.
Then A = QS 1 = π(−∆S 1 )1/2 .
Proof. Translation invariance + self-adjoint forces A to be a Fourier multiplier A = op(a(m))m∈Z .
The scale-phase lift to the Poisson semigroup gives a(m) = c|m| for some constant c. The
principal symbol π|ξ| fixes c = π. The normalization a(1) = π is consistent.
□
The corollary: every analytic Jordan curve Γ admits an exterior Riemann map Φ pulling Γ
back to S 1 , and the spectral Q-quadrature on Γ is the conformal pullback of the closed-form
spectral Q-rule on S 1 .
9. Numerical evidence
The point of the framework is that every analytic Jordan curve quadrature problem is the
same circle problem in disguise. Theorem 7.3 pulls Γ back to S 1 via the exterior Riemann map
Φ and decomposes the layer-potential kernel into the closed-form circle kernel log |wx − eiθc |
(eq. (30)) plus a real-analytic correction. The first part is precisely the operator on which
QS 1 = π(−∆S 1 )1/2 has closed-form spectrum (Theorem 2.2, Corollary 3.2) and an O(n log n)
functional calculus (Theorem 2.4); the second part is integrated at machine precision by the
standard trapezoidal rule on S 1 . The shape-dependent content is entirely contained in Φ —
specifically in its Laurent coefficients {ck }k≤1 and boundary correspondence θp (θc ) — both
precomputed once per geometry.

9.6. Comparison with QBX bounds. The sharp QBX error bound of Klinteberg-Tornberg
[11] is |EQBX (x)| ≤ CΓ τ (c, x)p+1 , where τ is the conformal ratio from expansion center c to
target x and p is the truncation order. For our flower geometry at δ = 10−6 , the conformal
ratio τ ≈ 0.4 (extracted from the Laurent coefficients of Φ at the nearest boundary point).
Achievable QBX bounds: p = 24 ⇒ |EQBX | ≈ 5 × 10−10 ; p = 32 ⇒ |EQBX | ≈ 5 × 10−13 .
The measured spectral-Q error at n = 2048 is 4.3 × 10−14 uniformly in δ ∈ [10−6 , 10−1 ],
beating the p = 32 QBX bound even in the most permissive QBX regime. The structural
advantage: QBX requires selecting expansion centers and orders adaptively per target to
maintain accuracy across the near-singular regime; the spectral-Q rule via kernel splitting has
no adaptive parameters — n and the boundary correspondence are fixed once per geometry.
9.7. Multiprecision certification. The kernel-splitting realization (Theorem 7.3, Definition 7.5) is arithmetic-agnostic: once the layer-potential problem is pulled back to S 1 , the
closed-form Fourier evaluation of the circle kernel and the trapezoidal evaluation of the analytic correction extend verbatim to arbitrary-precision arithmetic. A reference implementation
in the companion package GulatiLaplacian [22] drives the prototype across three precision
tiers — fp64, CPU multiprecision (mpmath, dps configurable), and an adaptive gate that
escalates from fp64 to multiprecision only when the two-grid estimator hits the fp64 floor.

9.8. BEM conditioning. The discrete operator Qn on equispaced nodes has condition number cond(Qn |1⊥ ) = λ⌊n/2⌋ /λ1 = O(n); the Galerkin-discretized Hadamard hypersingular operator W Gal on the same nodes has condition number O(n3 ) [12, 16]. Empirically, at n = 4096:
cond(Qn |1⊥ ) ≈ 2.0 × 103 (slope 1.00 in log log); cond(WnGal ) ≈ 1.6 × 1010 (slope 3.00). The
three-orders-of-magnitude conditioning advantage of Qn propagates to the boundary solve.
9.9. Algebraic identity tests. Three identities of Corollary 2.3 and Corollary 4.2 hold in
exact arithmetic: Qn 1 = 0 (verified ∥Qn 1∥∞ = 0 at n ∈ {212 , 216 , 218 , 220 }); detζ QS 1 =
2 (verified to 11 digits at n = 1024); ζQS1 (0) = −1 (exact). These confirm the algebraic
certification level of the framework.
10. Conclusion
The log-discriminant Hessian Q is a polynomial-algebraic object with closed-form spectrum
on S 1 , principal-symbol identification with πΛΓ on C 3 closed planar curves, and spectral zeta
limit Tn (s) → 2ζ(s). The spectral cone factorization of the Dirichlet spectrum into projective
shape coordinates times a radial scale axis enables algebraic recovery of shape and scale:
(λ1 , D2 λ1 ) determines Ω up to Euclidean motion on piecewise C 2,α domains; the truncated

spectrum determines shape to Hausdorff error K −1/2 (log K)c on real-analytic star-shaped
domains; area follows algebraically from the radial coordinate. The boundary quadrature
corollary attains the information-theoretic minimax floor on analytic Jordan curves via the
conformal pullback of S 1 , and matches Gauss-Legendre’s exponential rate structurally while
extending it to the boundary-integral setting.
The numerical evidence is calibrated across four shape classes (S 1 , smooth star-shaped,
smooth non-star-shaped, true-corner): machine precision uniformly in δ on S 1 ; fp64 machine
precision (∼ 4 × 10−14 ) uniformly in δ ∈ [10−6 , 10−1 ] on the 3-petal flower and perturbed
ellipse via Theorem 7.3 with polar Theodorsen; ∼ 6 × 10−7 uniformly on the teardrop via
arc-length Berrut; graceful algebraic degradation on the unit square preserving uniform-in-δ
behavior with 4–5× improvement over trapezoidal at δ = 10−6 and two orders of magnitude
improvement near a corner; algebraic identities certified to the fp64 floor.
