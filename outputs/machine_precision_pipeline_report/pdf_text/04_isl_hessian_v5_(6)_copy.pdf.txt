The point is not merely that a Hessian computation happens to produce an
inverse-square kernel. We prove that within the class of rotation-invariant, nonnegative difference forms on S 1 with exact linear spectrum λk = C|k|, the operator
and its generating interaction are unique up to normalization. The axioms are not
chosen; they are the minimal invariances required by Euclidean geometry.
The circle is the trivial exactly solvable case; a general curve retains only the
first-order Dirichlet-to-Neumann structure plus a bounded defect. The central point
of the paper is that ellipses are the first nontrivial curves for which that defect closes

This family matters because ellipses are the first nontrivial benchmark in several

the model family in many boundary spectral problems, and the first noncircular

claim to solve the global billiard-rigidity problem. Its contribution is more concrete and more explicit: it identifies a variationally generated boundary operator
for which the ellipse model can be written in exact closed form, and within that
model it separates three tasks cleanly: one-channel recovery of shape, one additional
normalization for scale, and full inverse-square kernel rigidity for congruence.

16. Exact Ellipse Hessian Law and Weighted DtN Comparison
The operator studied in this paper is introduced first through the Hessian law
for logarithmic interaction. On the circle it is the inverse-square Laplacian, and
there it satisfies
Q = π DtNS 1 = π (−∆)1/2 .
For a general curve Γ, the operator QΓ has the same first-order principal symbol
as π DtNΓ and differs from the flat model by a bounded zeroth-order term; see, for
example, Taylor [23], Stein [20], Caffarelli–Silvestre [5], McLean [16], Steinbach [22],
and Girouard–Polterovich [9]. Those classical constructions define the relevant
operator through boundary value problems or spectral functional calculus and then
derive its analytic properties.
The direction of the present paper is the reverse: we start from a concrete energy functional—the logarithmic pairwise interaction—and derive the operator as
an exact Hessian. The operator is not constructed or postulated; it emerges, with
no approximation, from the second variation of a natural energy. Accordingly, the
inverse-square Laplacian should be understood here as a canonical discrete realization of the fractional Laplacian induced by logarithmic interaction, not as an
assertion that it is the only possible discretization. From this perspective, the
Dirichlet-to-Neumann and fractional-Laplacian descriptions are continuum manifestations of the same Hessian operator rather than the starting point of the theory.
The main point is therefore not merely agreement of principal symbols, but an exact Hessian law and rigidity principle linking logarithmic interaction, the ISL, and
the Dirichlet-to-Neumann / fractional-Laplacian viewpoint. The properties listed
in items (i)–(iv) below are consequences of this variational mechanism rather than
of the standard analytic constructions.
Among general curves, ellipses occupy a distinguished place in this picture.
They are the first nontrivial geometries for which the bulk logarithmic Hessian,
the boundary inverse-square form, and the Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator can all
be compared explicitly in a single coordinate system. The weighted boundary kernel factorizes exactly, the finite-dimensional anomaly-coordinate Hessian already
splits into a difference channel and a midpoint channel, the Dirichlet-to-Neumann
operator separates into two integrable spectral channels, the bounded correction
Q∂E − πΛE admits an exact Bernoulli refinement, and exact matching of the full
inverse-square spectral data recovers the ellipse itself. The next group of results is
organized to make that ellipse model explicit.
(i) Variational origin. The inverse-square operator arises here as the exact second
variation of a pairwise interaction energy: δ 2 E id = −2Q, with kernel |z − w|−2 .
This provides a direct variational representation of the |ξ|-operator in terms of a
logarithmic interaction. To our knowledge, such an exact Hessian representation
does not appear in the standard DtN or fractional Laplacian constructions.
(ii) Exact kernel identification. While the fractional Laplacian is typically
defined abstractly—via Fourier multipliers or singular integrals [20], or via extension
problems [5]—here the kernel |z − w|−2 is obtained as the exact second derivative
of the logarithmic potential: g ′′ (α) = −1/(2 sin2 (α/2)). The singular kernel is not
postulated but derived, with no approximation or asymptotic argument.
(iii) Spectral rigidity. We prove that within the class of rotation-invariant,
nonnegative, constant-annihilating quadratic forms admitting a difference-kernel

THE GOLDEN ELLIPSE

59

representation and having exact linear spectrum λk = C|k|, the Fourier symbol is
uniquely determined (Theorem 6.1), and that the logarithmic kernel is the unique
periodic interaction whose Hessian generates it (Theorem 6.2). In contrast, the
fractional Laplacian is typically introduced as one member of a broader family
(−∆)s , 0 < s < 1 [5], without a characterization that singles out s = 1/2 from
symmetry and positivity alone.
(iv) Discrete-to-continuous unification. The same operator appears simultaneously in discrete and continuous settings and is linked by a convergence theorem
(Theorem 13.2). The discrete inverse-square Laplacians are not finite-difference
approximations imposed externally, but arise from the same energy functional and
converge naturally to the continuum operator.

Corollary 24.3 (Conditional global minimality on a compact elliptic slice). Let
U0 ⊂ U be a compact connected nondegenerate subset of the fixed-area elliptic pentagon chart from Theorem 24.2, and let JD be the area-normalized first Dirichlet
eigenvalue functional from (191). Assume:
(i) JD extends continuously to U0 and is C 2 on U0 ;
(ii) the regular pentagon Preg ∈ U0 is a critical point of JD ;
(iii) there exists κ > 0 such that
D2 JD (Preg )[u, u] ≥ κ QPreg (u, u)

(u ∈ TPreg U );

(iv) Preg is the only critical point of JD in U0 ;
(v) JD (P ) > JD (Preg ) for every P ∈ ∂U0 .
Then Preg is the unique global minimizer of JD on U0 . In particular, since |ΩP | =
A0 on U0 , the regular pentagon is also the unique minimizer of λD
1 (ΩP ) on U0 .
If, in addition, the Q-gradient flow of JD leaves U0 forward invariant, then every
forward trajectory in U0 converges to Preg .
Proof. Because U0 is compact and JD is continuous, JD attains a minimum at
some point Pmin ∈ U0 . Assumption (v) excludes the boundary, so Pmin ∈ U0 .
Every interior minimizer is a critical point, hence by (iv) one has Pmin = Preg . This
proves the global minimality and uniqueness statement.
By Theorem 24.2, assumption (iii) implies that Preg is a strict local minimizer
and a locally exponentially stable equilibrium of the Q-gradient flow. Now assume
U0 is forward invariant. Along every forward trajectory P (t) ⊂ U0 , (190) shows
that JD (P (t)) is decreasing and bounded below, hence convergent. Since U0 is
compact, every trajectory has a nonempty ω-limit set. Standard LaSalle invariance
for smooth gradient systems on compact sets implies that every ω-limit point is
a critical point of JD . By (iv), the only such point is Preg . Therefore the whole
trajectory converges to Preg .
