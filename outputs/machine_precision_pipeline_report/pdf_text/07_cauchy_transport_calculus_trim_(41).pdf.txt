An Inverse-Square Boundary Laplacian for Near-Singular Quadrature
Rick Gulati
University of Pennsylvania
rkgulati@upenn.edu

Abstract
Boundary-integral methods represent the solution of an elliptic problem as a layer potential — an integral over the
boundary against an unknown density. Evaluating it at points near the boundary, the close-evaluation problem, has been
the standing bottleneck of the field for four decades: a fixed high-order quadrature rule is accurate far away but loses all
of its digits once the target lies within one node spacing of the boundary, where the kernel becomes sharply peaked.
We show that this difficulty is governed by a single intrinsic operator on the curve — the inverse-square boundary
Laplacian Q, the graph Laplacian whose weight between two boundary points is the inverse square of the distance
between them — and that Q is the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator up to a bounded term. On the circle Q is
circulant with the integer spectrum λm = m(n − m)/2, applied by the FFT in O(n log n) time and conditioned as O(n),
against O(n3 ) for regularized hypersingular assembly. Diagonalizing Q evaluates the layer potential as a modal sum in
which the target enters only through one scalar, so on analytic data the rule reaches machine precision uniformly in the
distance to the boundary — verified to 50 digits and carried to non-circular curves by a closed-form conformal pullback.
Because the spectrum is an exact quadratic, the same closed form inverts the discrete-to-continuum defect exactly,
curing near-resonance Helmholtz (wave) and Steklov evaluation — and, through the same radial channel multipliers,
the screened, diffusive heat problem — and the construction extends through a corner by a Hurwitz-zeta correction. In
a direct comparison with quadrature by expansion the fixed rule is distance-uniform where QBX degrades as the target
approaches, is competitive at the closest distances at a fraction of the nodes, and stays defined at cusps where the QBX
expansion centre does not exist. Because the rule is one differentiable linear map with no mesh and no case-by-case
branching, it can be differentiated end to end, turning boundary shape optimization into plain gradient descent with no
remeshing as the shape moves. The distance-uniformity is a property of the analytic class, and we fence it as such.

2.1. Near-singular quadrature: four families
On-curve specialized and corrected quadratures. Kress
logarithmic splitting [19], the Kapur–Rokhlin corrected
trapezoidal rules [17], and Alpert’s hybrid Gauss–

order by augmenting the trapezoidal rule with a small set
of end-corrected nodes whose weights are precomputed
against the logarithmic singularity. They are designed for
the on-curve integral and degrade off it, because the analytic splitting of the on-curve singularity does not extend
to a nearby exterior target.

Gauss–Legendre is the natural challenger: it is the classically optimal rule for the interval problem, and panelbased Gauss–Legendre is the workhorse of boundaryelement practice. With n nodes it is exact on polynomials

3. The operator behind the breakdown

The benchmark for optimality is supplied by approximation theory, not by comparison with the previous rule.
For a fixed smoothness class F the worst-case error of
the best n-point rule is, up to the choice of admissible
information, a Gel’fand width of the layer-potential functional restricted to F [26, 23]; the theory of n-widths
identifies the worst-case error of the best n-point rule
with a width of the unit ball of F under the functional
and characterizes the rules that attain it. For analytic
classes the relevant geometry is potential-theoretic: the
optimal nodes are the Fekete points of Γ for the exterior
equilibrium measure, and the attainable rate is governed
by the conformal modulus [30]. The framework of optimal recovery and information-based complexity [25]
fixes the sense in which a rule is best: it minimizes the
radius of information over the class.

4. Dirichlet-to-Neumann identification
On a general curve Q loses its circulant form but keeps
its symbolic identity. We establish the identification in
full, and are deliberately economical about the machinery:
the only property of the remainder that any later inference
uses is that it is a bounded self-adjoint operator on L2 ,
and that is exactly what we prove — at C2 regularity, by
an elementary bounded-kernel estimate, with no appeal
to a classical (polyhomogeneous) symbol expansion. The
stronger statement that the remainder is a classical orderzero pseudodifferential operator holds when Γ ∈ C∞ , but
it is not needed and we do not assume it (Remark 4.8).

8.1. Restored-order convergence
Subtracting the rungs below a chosen order lifts the rate
from the corner-limited λmin = minq π/αq to the regularity of what remains. In the tropical envelope the vertex channel adds the barrier Bvtx = λmin log(1/h) + O(1),
algebraic in 1/h and hence dominant near a re-entrant
corner (λmin < 1) until corrected.

Corners thus no longer cap the order, which is set by
the correction level Λ and the Sobolev regularity of the
regular density. The corner DtN operator itself (the offdiagonal coupling of the two incident faces) is a separate
object not required for the quadrature problem; what
near-singular evaluation needs is exactly the trapezoidal
defect of the singular density, in which geometry fixes the
exponent and the Hurwitz value converts it to a correction.

the operator is assembled from pairwise chords without a mesh, run by the smooth FFT calculus along each

10. Differentiability and meshless shape optimization
The structural facts that make Q a good evaluator — a
single fixed linear map (an FFT, a diagonal multiply by a
closed-form symbol, an inverse FFT, and a closed-form
local term), with no mesh, no quadrature weights to retune, and no per-target expansion centre or case split
— have a consequence beyond accuracy: the map is a
smooth function of the boundary node positions, and its
derivative is elementary. Reading the operator as the
Hessian of the log-discriminant (§1) makes this concrete:
every entry (Q)i j = −|zi − z j |−2 is a smooth, branch-free
function of the coordinates, so any scalar built from Q —
its quadratic form, its Gram matrix, a spectral functional
— is smooth in the shape, and reverse-mode automatic
differentiation returns the exact gradient at the cost of one
extra application.

11. Hearing the shape of a drum
Kac’s question — whether the Laplace spectrum determines a planar domain — has a known negative answer:
Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert exhibited non-isometric polygons with identical Dirichlet Laplace spectra [1, 2]. On
such a pair the Laplacian is provably blind. The exact
Laplace spectral difference is identically zero (Figure 14,
top right, the flat line at the axis); any nonzero difference
a computation reports stays at the discretization-artifact
level and refines away (the low finite-difference curve),
not geometry. The boundary operator Q is a different
invariant: it reads the chord geometry of the boundary
rather than the interior eigenvalue problem (§4), and on
the same pair it is not blind.

Two conductors: coupling at fixed area. Figure 12
maximizes the electrostatic coupling between two conductors Γ1 , Γ2 — a functional of the inter-body Q energy
— subject to each enclosing a fixed area. The objective
rises from −43.5 to −29.3 while both areas stay pinned

close the minimum gap from 1.00 to 0.36 and raise the

12. Conclusion
One operator runs through the paper: the inverse-square
chord Laplacian Q, the Hessian of the log-discriminant
along the boundary, with its kernel forced rather than
chosen (Proposition 3.1, Lemma 6.1). Three identifications make it useful for near-singular quadrature. It
is a graph Laplacian with closed-form integer spectrum
λm = m(n − m)/2 on the circle, giving an O(n log n) FFT
functional calculus and O(n) conditioning where regularized hypersingular assembly costs O(n3 ) (§3, §7). It is, on
any C1,1 curve, the boundary Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator up to a bounded self-adjoint term, Q = πΛΓ + K0 ,
established by an elementary estimate together with the
resulting Weyl law (§4). And because its spectrum is an
exact quadratic in the mode, the continuum

What the spectral separation does and does not claim.
The experiment of §11 shows that the inverse-square
spectrum distinguishes a pair of domains the Dirichlet
Laplacian provably cannot. It does not show that the
Q-spectrum determines a shape, and we are careful not
to claim the stronger statement — that equal Q-spectra
force congruent domains — even though the evidence
points that way. The separation we observe is exactly
what a complete invariant would produce, but one separating example is not completeness. We therefore leave the
rigidity question open, for later work, rather than assert
it. Settling it would require the continuum Q-spectrum,
control of the polygonal vertex channel in that limit, and
an inverse-spectral reconstruction of the boundary from
the chord operator — none of which the analytic-class
fence of this paper supplies.
