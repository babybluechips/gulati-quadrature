Literature and Continuation Problem

2.1

Classical Starting Point

conditional theorem
rigidity theorem

Kneser constructed a real-analytic solution of the exponential iteration problem, including the
functional square root of the exponential and related fractional iterates. In modern notation, the
goal is a super-exponential F or tetration branch satisfying
F (h + 1) = exp(F (h)),

F (0) = 1,

with real analyticity on the real height axis and the Kneser symmetry/strip construction in the
complex plane.
Szekeres developed regular iteration for real and complex functions, emphasizing that Abel functions are not unique without an additional regularity principle. Koenigs and Schroder linearization
give local fractional iteration near a nonneutral fixed point:
T [h] (z) = σ −1 (λh σ(z)).
This local formula is indispensable, but it leaves choices of fixed point, logarithm sheet, and continuation domain.

2.2

Modern Uniqueness and Computation

Trappmann and Kouznetsov gave uniqueness criteria for holomorphic Abel functions at a complex
fixed-point pair and showed that Kneser’s Abel function satisfies such a criterion. Paulsen and
Cowgill later gave natural conditions that force Kneser’s solution to be unique for generalized
tetration in the complex plane and developed high-accuracy numerical methods.
The state of the literature is therefore:
(L1) Kneser gives the classical real-analytic branch for base e.
(L2) Szekeres and regular iteration explain why an Abel function requires a regularity principle.
3

(L3) Koenigs/Schroder theory gives local fractional iteration but not a global canonical branch by
itself.
(L4) Trappmann–Kouznetsov and Paulsen–Cowgill give complex uniqueness criteria and computational access to Kneser-type branches.
The present paper adds a geometric/operator invariant to this line: the branch-free Joukowski
eccentricity value and its unique normalization.

3.4

Layer 3: Continuum and Operator Height

The continuum layer treats height as a genuine flow parameter, not merely a label. The infinitesimal
generator

zeta, killed semigroups, and spectral fractional powers: once the flow coordinate is additive, continuum operations in the height variable become canonical.

Principle 3.1 (Canonical Continuation Principle). The canonical analytic continuation of Kneser
tetration is the continuation that preserves, at every layer, the additive coordinate in which composition becomes translation, and whose surviving branch-free scalar is the Joukowski eccentricity
readout.
Principle 3.2 (Certified Complex-Height Value). At a genuinely complex height, a tetration value
is canonical only if it is observable from the all-order Abel-flow jet tower in a way that is invariant
under logarithm sheets, Abel-coordinate reparametrizations preserving integer iteration, reciprocal
Galois lifts, and auxiliary scale choices. Thus the value is not an arbitrary point of a local Schroder
chart; it is the unique branch-free scalar surviving the Kneser continuation data.
Axiom 3.3 (Physical-Space Realizability). The surviving scalar of a reasonable complex-height
tetration must have a physical-space meaning: after branch-free Abel descent, the reciprocal scale
R must be realized as an actual Euclidean conic in a real plane, with no auxiliary scale, no chosen
focus, and no coordinate-dependent measurement. The value assigned to the complex height is the
primitive dimensionless Euclidean focal observable of that conic.
